Episode 003 — Location

New Location, New Story

Episode 003 shoots against the most visually complex backdrop in the series so far. The painted mural wall on Kofi Oku Road — a salmon and sienna Accra skyline silhouette — is not just a background. It is a deliberate act of civic art in S May's neighbourhood. Town D speaks through colour. The wall is Jamestown asserting itself.

Episode 002 — Bla Quarter
Comodor Street Arch — Interior Compound, Terracotta
Semi-interior. Deep terracotta. Arch shadow and compound darkness. Private threshold between family life and the street. Darker, more enclosed world.
Episode 003 — S May
Kofi Oku Road — Mural Wall, Salmon & Sienna Skyline
Exterior or semi-exterior. Painted salmon wall with deep sienna Accra skyline silhouette mural. Civic art as backdrop. More ambient light than E002 arch but still warm and textured. The street-facing world — visible, public, communal.

Location Opportunity — The Mural Frame

The painted Accra skyline behind S May is extraordinary found production design. Position her so the mural's city silhouette rises behind her — she becomes the living figure against the painted city. The sienna buildings and structures echo Jamestown's own built environment. She is literally sitting in front of the city her generation built and inhabited. Do not position her to block it — let it breathe behind her.

Mural Reading
The mural features a cross/church motif visible in the painted skyline. Given S May's connection to Accra Wesley Cathedral (Methodist), this is not incidental — it may be thematically significant. Note its position relative to her framing on shoot day.
Colour Palette — What Changes From E002
E002 was deep terracotta in shadow. E003 is salmon-warm in ambient light. The wall is a fresher, more vivid surface — paint applied more recently, more saturated. The grade should be warm but brighter than E002. Town D feels more exposed than Comodor Street's compound interior. Grade accordingly.

Born on 25th January 1965 on Kofi Oku Road, Town D, Salomay Naa Lamley Lawson — known as S May — has spent 61 years in Jamestown without leaving. She is a trader. She is a cook. She is the head cook for the Lante Dzan We feast — one of the most significant communal meals in Jamestown's Homowo cycle. The Lawson family participates in community service. Her faith is rooted in Accra Wesley Cathedral, the seat of Ghanaian Methodism. Her school — Akoto Lante Primary — is the setting for one of this episode's most extraordinary stories.

What makes S May's voice singular in this series is the range she holds in one body: the intimacy of family food preparation, the weight of a community tragedy, and the discipline of tradition handed forward. She does not perform her history. She carries it in her hands.

Why This Voice Matters

The Well Story
The story of the school children who fell into the well at the Akoto Lante house is not just a story — it is a warning and a lesson about safety, responsibility and community care. This episode holds it. Hearing it from someone who witnessed or lived through it makes the lesson powerful. This is the episode's anchor. Handle it with the same care you would give any account of loss.
The Lawson Family — Community Service
The Lawson family's participation in community service is part of S May's inherited identity. This is not civic volunteerism — it is cultural obligation. Explore what that means in Ga terms: what the community asked of this family, and what the family gave.

Key Stories to Draw Out

Story 1
The Homowo Festival — its full meaning, its rhythms, its food, its gathering. What it felt like in Jamestown's glory days vs. what it feels like now.
Story 2
Lost traditions and values — specific things that have disappeared from Jamestown that she witnessed in their prime. What was lost and when.
Story 3 — ANCHOR
The well at Akoto Lante Primary — the school children who fell. A true life event. Approach with care. Let her lead the pace. Do not rush this story.
Story 4
The Lante Dzan We feast — S May as head cook. What goes into Kpekple. What it means to be the one who feeds the community on the most sacred day.
Director's Note — Medium Comfort on Camera
S May's comfort level is medium. Unlike E001 (reserved) and E002 (high comfort), this requires a calibrated approach. Build rapport before cameras roll. Do not rush to the difficult stories — let her find her footing first on the Homowo questions, which are joyful. The well story should come only once she has settled into the interview. Fluent Ga-speaking director is non-negotiable.
Episode 003 — The Central Story

The Well

The story of the children who fell into the well at the Akoto Lante house is not a story S May tells to be heard. It is a story she carries because she has no choice. It happened in her community. It happened at a place she knows — the school she attended. And she has not forgotten. This is the structural and emotional centre of Episode 003. Everything else in the episode — the feast, the festival, the faith — forms the world in which this tragedy is held.

What We Know

School children fell into a well at the Akoto Lante house. S May witnessed or lived through it. It is a true life event. She attended Akoto Lante Primary.

Beyond that: we do not know the full details until she tells them. We do not know how many children. We do not know what she saw or heard. We do not know whether there were deaths. We do not know how old she was. This is what the interview exists to hold. Do not speculate before shoot day. Do not brief the crew on assumptions. Let the story arrive in the room as she brings it.

Interview Approach — Protocol
When to Approach It

Not first. Not second. The well story must come in Act 2, after S May has been in the room for at least 30–40 minutes, after the Homowo and Lante Dzan We questions have warmed the space, after she has laughed or smiled at something. The director must feel the room before opening this door. If the atmosphere is not right — wait. The interview can run long. The story will be there.

How to Open It

One question. In Ga. Simple and direct — do not frame it in advance, do not prepare her for gravity. Something like: "Tell me about the well at Akoto Lante." Then: silence. Do not elaborate. Do not add context. Do not signal that you know anything. Let her decide what she tells and in what order. Her pace is the pace of the scene.

While She Tells It

No crew movement. No prompts. No affirming sounds from the director — not a "yes," not an "mm." Silence and presence. The director's only tool is stillness. If she pauses — wait. Count silently to eight before considering a prompt. The pauses are the story. If she stops and does not continue, a single Ga word of gentle acknowledgement is permitted. Nothing more.

After She Finishes

Hold silence for at least 10 seconds on her face. Do not ask a follow-up immediately. Let the room breathe. If she needs water or a moment — give it without ceremony. Do not treat her differently afterward; the most respectful thing is to continue the interview naturally, not to make the moment feel set apart in a way that isolates her.

As Community History

Oral history carries the events that do not appear in newspaper archives, that families hold privately, that communities process through memory rather than record. The well is one of those events. S May's testimony may be the only recorded account of it that exists. This film is the archive.

As Safety & Responsibility

The bio sheet is explicit: the tragedy is a warning and a lesson about safety, responsibility, and community care. In the context of a community development documentary, this is also a living question — who is responsible for the children of Jamestown today? The well asks it across sixty years.

As the Episode's Weight

The feast and the well are the two poles of this episode: the joy of feeding a community and the grief of failing to protect its children. Neither negates the other. S May holds both. The edit must honour that she carries both truths simultaneously — not as a contrast, but as the full complexity of a life lived in one place for sixty-one years.

Camera & Editorial Directives — The Well Sequence Only
A-Cam
Locked off on her face. Do not reframe during the story. Whatever frame was set when she begins — hold it. Do not zoom in for dramatic effect. If her eyes fill — hold. If she looks away — hold. The camera does not chase. It witnesses.
B-Cam
Wide frame — subject and mural wall. Hold. Do not cut to B-cam during the well story in the edit. It is coverage, not a design choice. The edit stays on A-cam for the full account. B-cam becomes available for the moment immediately before and immediately after.
Sound
When the well story begins — the sound design team pulls all ambient beds to near silence. Her voice is the only sound. When she reaches the gravity of what happened — absolute silence under her voice. No music. No ambient. This is the one moment in the episode where silence is not a design choice. It is an obligation.
Editorial
No cutaways during the account. No B-roll of the school, the road, the well — not during her telling. Those images, if used, belong before or after, never under her voice. After she finishes: hold on her face for no fewer than 8 seconds. Then black. Then the ambient street sound returns. Then the interview continues.
B-Roll Research
If the well at the Akoto Lante house still exists or its location is known — document it on B-roll before or after the shoot. Exterior only. No dramatisation. A still image of where it stood, or the space it has become, may be the most powerful cut in the episode. Ask S May if she is willing to direct you to it. Do not push.

"The well is not a metaphor. It is a place. Children fell. S May remembers. The film's responsibility is to hold that memory with the same care she has carried it — without dramatisation, without sentimentality, and without looking away."

Jamestown Glory Days — Mission

Youth Impact Framework

S May's story carries a specific and urgent message for Jamestown's youth — that tradition is not abstract. It lives in the hands that cook the food, in the voices that sing the festivals, and in the grief of those who remember what safety means when it fails.

Episode 003 · Youth Mission Statement
The Feast Is a Responsibility. The Well Is a Warning.
S May is the head cook for the Lante Dzan We feast. She learned this from the women before her. Someone taught her what goes into Kpekple. Someone taught her when to stir and when to wait. Someone taught her that the feast is not for one family — it is for everyone. The youth of Jamestown are in danger of letting that chain break. Not from rebellion, but from forgetting. And in the same community, children once fell into a well that everyone knew was there. That story is a question: who is responsible for the safety of the children? The answer is the same answer as who is responsible for feeding them at Homowo. Everyone. Always.

The Food Thread
Ask S May to describe making Kpekple from first step to last. This is not a recipe — it is a cultural act. Let her hands be in frame if she begins to gesture the motions of cooking. Keep cameras rolling.
The Loss Thread
Ask her to name one tradition that has disappeared completely from Jamestown in her lifetime. Just one. The specificity of the loss is more powerful than a catalogue.
End Card Direction
Final question: "What do you want the young people of Jamestown to carry forward — from the feast, and from the children you remember?" Her answer is the episode's closing frame. Direct address to youth. Hold on face 12 seconds.
Episode 003 — Story Arc

Three-Part Mini-Arc

A woman who has fed a community for sixty years, watched its children play — and remembered the day the ground gave way beneath them.

S May's arc follows the series template — Past, Faith/Family/Community, Legacy/Youth — but structured around the specific weight of her stories: the Homowo festival as the spine, the Kpekple feast as the living tradition she embodies, and the well as the gravity that anchors the whole.

The salmon mural wall is vivid and saturated — brighter than E001's ochre or E002's terracotta. The challenge is separating the subject cleanly from a warm, medium-value painted surface. As a female subject from a Methodist background, S May may own ankara or church fabric in deeply appropriate colours.

Wardrobe Conversation Protocol
Do not tell S May what to wear. Show her two or three colour options and let her choose. As a Ga elder and Wesley Cathedral member she likely owns ankara or kente-print fabric in appropriate colours. A church Sunday cloth in deep teal, navy, or forest green is culturally authentic and cinematically ideal. A headtie (duku) in a complementary colour adds visual height and cultural authenticity. She was wearing a black top at the location recce — black reads powerfully against the salmon wall and should be offered as an option.

Golden Hour · Kofi Oku Road Mural Wall

Camera & Light Schematic

The mural wall offers a fundamentally different challenge from E002's arch. There is no deep shadow interior — the location is exterior or semi-exterior, with ambient light raking the painted wall directly. The visual tension is between the salmon-orange wall surface and the deeper sienna mural silhouette. The subject must read off both layers. The mural city behind her is the visual centrepiece — frame accordingly.

Post-Production

Sound Design

For E003 specifically: the Homowo festival is one of the most sonically rich traditions in Ga culture. If S May describes or re-enacts any aspect of the Lante Dzan We feast, the sound design team must research and source authentic Homowo music and ambient recordings. The well story requires a moment of absolute silence — this must be protected in the mix.

1
Dialogue Bed
Clean, present, intimate. Boom primary; lav as emergency fill. Exterior mural wall is more open than E002 arch — less reverb concern, more ambient noise management.
Peaks at −18dBFS; 12dB headroom. Monitor Kofi Oku Road traffic — may require real-time management.
2
Kofi Oku Road Ambient
Town D street sounds — different character from Owuman and Comodor Street. This is E003's sonic fingerprint. The street under the mural. Market calls, children, coastal birds.
Independent ambient recorder running all day. Loop crossfaded under all sequences.
3
Homowo Festival — Sound Research
Homowo has specific drumming, the sound of Kpekple preparation, communal singing. Source authentic recordings or commission with Ga musicians. This is the sonic centrepiece of the episode.
Research before shoot day. Consult S May — she may know where festival recordings exist.
4
Wesley Cathedral Acoustics
When she speaks of Accra Wesley Cathedral — a subtle long-decay stone reverb opens under her voice. Wesley has a very specific acoustic character — the cathedral IR matters.
Use an appropriate Anglican/Methodist cathedral IR. Pull back as she returns to street life.
5
The Well — Absolute Silence
When the well story reaches its gravity — the moment of the fall — the mix must be capable of absolute silence. All ambient beds fade. Her voice alone. Do not underestimate this editorial moment.
Design the silence in post before the mix. The absence of sound is the sound design choice here.
6
Memory Washes
4–8 bar motif from series composer. The Homowo season motif may inform this episode's specific version. Brief at 10% under dialogue; rises only after she stops speaking.
Maximum 3–4 uses per episode. The well story: no music under it. None.
7
Series Motif — E003 Version
The 4-note Jamestown motif returns from E001 and E002, but in E003 it may incorporate a percussive element drawn from Homowo drumming tradition. Composer decision.
Full resolution in Part III closing — as she finishes her direct address to youth. Then the motif. Then black.
8
Youth Address End Card
"The feast is for you. The warning is for you." — the final frame. Dry mix, full presence, no reverb. S May speaking directly to the viewer. No music under her voice.
Underscore fades completely. Her voice alone. Then the series motif — and black.

Editorial Principles

All series editorial principles apply to E003. Additional E003 note: the well story is the most emotionally grave material in the series to date. The editor must approach it with the same care the director used in the interview — no cutaways during the account, no music under it, no rush away from S May's face after she finishes.

The Held Cut
"Hold interview frames 1.5× longer than instinct says."
Emotion lives in the face after words end. Cutting too early wastes the most valuable footage.
The Breath Cut
"Cut on the exhale, not the inhale."
Follows the natural rhythm of speech. Cuts on exhales feel invisible to the viewer.
The Silence Cut
"Never cut during a pause — wait for the next word, then cut back."
Pauses are the subject thinking. Cutting mid-pause breaks the viewer's connection to that thought.
The Well Sequence
"Do not cut during the well account. No music. No cutaways. Face only."
This is testimony about a community tragedy. The editor's discipline is to not let the discomfort of the material drive cuts. Hold. The viewer must feel the weight of what S May carries.
Kpekple Sequence
"If she demonstrates cooking — let her hands stay in frame. Do not cut to her face prematurely."
Hands in motion over food are a visual act of cultural transmission. The cooking IS the story. Stay on it.
Youth End Card
"Every episode ends with the elder's direct address to Jamestown's youth."
This is the series mission made explicit. It is not optional. It is the reason we are making this.
Music Entry
"Music enters at 10% under dialogue and rises only after the subject stops speaking."
Music must never compete with the elder's voice. Her words are primary.
Episode Endings
"Every episode ends on minimum 8 seconds of black."
Allow the audience to sit with what they heard before anything else demands their attention.
