Episode 004 — Location

New Location, New Story

Episode 004 is the most tonally distinct location in the series so far. The white lime-washed compound wall at Kofi Oku Road is not a street-facing mural or a public arch — it is a private domestic threshold. Chalk white. A dark green louvred window. A tiled ledge with potted plants. An open white door. This is the interior world of Jamestown. Quiet, domestic, alive with the details of a life actually lived.

Episode 003 — S May
Kofi Oku Road — Salmon Mural Wall, Public Street
Exterior. Vivid civic art. Salmon and sienna. Public-facing. The neighbourhood asserting itself outward. High saturation, warm amber light.
Episode 004 — Hajia 1 Day
Kofi Oku Road — White Lime Compound, Private Threshold
Semi-interior. Chalk white textured plaster. Deep shadow interior behind the open door. Dark green louvre window. Terracotta tile and warm wood. The most minimal, tonal palette in the series — the drama is in the restraint.

Location Opportunity — The White Wall

The white compound wall is the most cinematographically demanding location in the series. White reads as overexposed if not managed — but properly exposed, it becomes extraordinary. The texture of the lime plaster catches light directionally. The chalk surface has character — it is not paint-fresh white, it is lived-in white. The dark interior of the open doorway on the right creates a natural shadow anchor. Position Hajia 1 Day between the wall and the doorway so the darkness frames her on one side.

The Louvre Window
The deep green louvred window above and behind the subject is exceptional found production design. Its dark green grid against white plaster is one of the most visually distinctive frames in the series. If the subject is seated on or near the ledge, the window becomes a compositional element above her — weight and history in the background.
Potted Plants — Found Accent
The two white cement pots with green plants on the tiled ledge are alive — they should remain in frame if they are present on shoot day. Green against white against shadow is the episode's three-tone palette in one composition. Do not move them. They are part of the location's domestic life, not props to be cleared.
Colour Palette — What Changes From E003
E003 was vivid salmon at high saturation. E004 is the opposite — chalk white at near-zero saturation. This is the most tonally restrained episode in the series. The grade will work with very little colour information. The visual drama comes from the deep shadow interior (the open door), the sage-green window, and the warm terracotta tile underfoot. Skin tones against white become the sole warm element in the frame.

Born in August 1973 on Kofi Oku Road, Town D, Mary Moodi — known everywhere in Jamestown as Hajia 1 Day — is a pork seller and cook. She cooks during the Homowo festival for the Lantey Dzan-We. She is 53. She has raised seven children from one mother and one father. She belongs to True Word Ministry at Agbado Bus Stop. She has no formal education on record. And she has watched Jamestown's fishing economy — its men, its women, its smoke, its salt — from the inside.

What makes Hajia 1 Day's voice irreplaceable in this series is proximity. She did not observe the fishing world from a distance. She lived in it. She cooked beside the women who smoked fish over open fires. She knew the fishermen who went to sea each morning. She watched the youth of Jamestown change. She carries those three layers — the sea, the women, the shift — in one life on one road.

The Nickname — Hajia 1 Day

Ask About the Name
The nickname "Hajia 1 Day" is not incidental. Names in Jamestown carry stories — they are given for reasons, confirmed by events, and held for decades. Ask her, in Ga, what the name means. How it came. Who gave it. This may be the interview's most surprising open door. Let her take it wherever it leads.

Key Stories to Draw Out

Story 1
The difference in attitude between the youth of Jamestown then and now — what changed, what was lost, what she observes in the young people on her street today.
Story 2 — The Fishermen
The life of the fishermen who went to sea daily. What that departure looked like each morning. What the return looked like. What the sea took and what it gave back.
Story 3 — The Women
Women smoking and selling fish. Who those women were. What the smoke smelled like. What their work meant for the economy of Jamestown and for the dignity of women in trade.
Story 4
The Lantey Dzan-We — her role in the Homowo cooking. What it meant to cook for the community feast as a woman whose faith is True Word Ministry, not traditional religion.
Mobility Consideration
Mobility is noted as a physical consideration. Confirm seating arrangements well in advance — the production manager should check with her before shoot day. The compound ledge in the location photo offers a natural, comfortable, low seating option that does not require navigating steps. Confirm this works for her. Have a proper chair available as backup. Do not place her in a position that creates physical strain during a 90-minute interview.
Director's Note — Medium Comfort, Tighter Window
Hajia 1 Day's comfort level is medium, and her availability window is the tightest in the series — after 4pm on most days, not available Monday or Tuesday. This means the golden hour window and the interview window are nearly identical. Do not let the time pressure affect the rapport-building. Arrive early, set up early, and have everything ready before she arrives so the transition to camera is effortless.
Jamestown Glory Days — Mission

Youth Impact Framework

Hajia 1 Day's stories carry a direct message for the youth of Jamestown about labour, dignity, and the economics of a community that fed itself from the sea. The fishing world she describes is not history — it is the foundation that the present stands on, mostly without knowing it.

Episode 004 · Youth Mission Statement
The Women Who Smoked the Fish Fed This Town.
Before the supermarkets. Before the frozen imports. Before the phones. There were women on the beach at Jamestown who woke before sunrise, built fires, smoked fish over heat until their hands and hair and clothes carried the smell of it, and sold what they made to feed the neighbourhood. Those women had no titles. They did not appear in newspapers. They were not celebrated. They simply worked — and the community ate. Hajia 1 Day knew those women. She can name them. She can describe the colour of the smoke. The youth of Jamestown are descendants of that economy. They should know what it cost, and what it was worth.
The Fishing Economy Women in Trade Youth & Attitude The Sea Homowo & Lantey Dzan-We Dignity of Labour Community Memory
The Sea Thread
Ask her to describe one morning when the fishermen left for the sea. Not a general description — one specific morning she remembers. The hour. The sound. Who she saw. The specificity of the memory is the teaching.
The Women Thread
Ask her to name one woman who smoked fish on the Jamestown beach. Just one. With her name. This act of naming — of refusing to let a person be anonymous — is the series mission at its most precise.
End Card Direction
Final question: "What do you want the young people of Jamestown to understand about the women who worked on this beach?" Her answer is the episode's closing frame. Direct address to youth. Hold 12 seconds. Fade to black.

Hajia 1 Day's arc follows the series template — Past, Faith/Family/Community, Legacy/Youth — built around the fishing world as the spine. The pork trade and the Homowo cooking are the personal threads. The youth and attitude shift is the judgment she brings to the present.

Colour Separation Strategy

The white lime wall is the most demanding wardrobe challenge in the series. White-on-white disappears entirely. The subject must carry the colour of the frame. This is the episode where fabric choice is most critical. Deep, saturated colour against white reads beautifully — the subject becomes the painting in front of the wall.

Wardrobe Conversation Protocol
Do not tell Hajia 1 Day what to wear. Show her two or three colour options and let her choose. As a woman connected to Jamestown's trading and cooking culture she likely owns ankara or kente-print fabric. A deep teal, navy, or rich brown church cloth reads beautifully against the white wall. A headtie in a complementary colour adds visual height. This is also the episode where black fabric would be most striking cinematically — the subject as pure silhouette against chalk white plaster.

Golden Hour · Kofi Oku Road White Compound

Camera & Light Schematic

The white compound wall inverts every assumption from the previous three episodes. E001–E003 were about managing warm-toned walls that absorbed light. E004 is about managing a surface that reflects almost everything. The white plaster is the most aggressive bounce source on set. The open doorway — with its deep interior shadow — is the episode's only natural anchor point.

WHITE WALL [ chalk lime plaster · louvre window above · open door right ] ^ [ DARK GREEN LOUVRE WINDOW — visual anchor above subject ] [ SHADOW INTERIOR — open door, right side — depth anchor ] [ SUBJECT — Hajia 1 Day ] positioned mid-frame · wall behind · door right [ COMPOUND LEDGE — seating option ] | [ NEGATIVE FILL (black wrap or flag) — camera-right ] Prevents white wall bounce from filling shadow side completely [ GOLDEN SUN — rakes wall from the west at golden hour ] White plaster catches directional light: texture becomes visible [ LED KEY · diffused · camera-left · MINIMAL output ] The wall is your fill. The LED supplements only at low levels. [ A-CAM · BMPCC 6K · 85mm · f/2.2 · on sticks ] [ B-CAM · Lumix S5 · 85mm · f/2.0 · 30° offset ] [ KEY: ETTR · zebras at 100 IRE · protect wall ] [ the door shadow is depth · the wall texture is character ]
E004 Key Challenge
White wall blows highlights easily and fills shadows aggressively. Use negative fill to retain face contrast. Expose for skin tone and let the wall live at the top of the range — just under blown.
Louvre Window Use
The dark green louvre window above the subject is a natural compositional anchor. Frame at 50mm to show it. At golden hour, any warm light through the louvres onto the wall creates organic pattern — do not flag it. Let it breathe.

Colour Grade Direction

E004 is the inverse grade challenge from all previous episodes. E001–E003 managed warm walls and skin tones in warm ambient. E004 has a near-neutral white wall, a cooler ambient at 5500K, and two small accent colours — sage green and terracotta wood. The grade must find warmth in a cool frame. The subject's skin tones are the only warm primary element. Everything else steps back.

Editorial Principles

All series editorial principles apply to E004. Additional E004 note: if Hajia 1 Day names a fish-smoking woman — if she uses a name — that name should be held in the edit. It should be the cut point. The edit pauses on the name. This is the series mission made most personal: not community memory in general, but one specific woman's name, said aloud, preserved.

The Held Cut
"Hold interview frames 1.5× longer than instinct says."
Emotion lives in the face after words end. Cutting too early wastes the most valuable footage.
The Breath Cut
"Cut on the exhale, not the inhale."
Follows the natural rhythm of speech. Cuts on exhales feel invisible to the viewer.
The Silence Cut
"Never cut during a pause — wait for the next word, then cut back."
Pauses are the subject thinking. Cutting mid-pause breaks the viewer's connection to that thought.
The Named Woman
"If she names a fish-smoking woman — hold on her face after the name. Do not cut."
A name spoken aloud in this film is an act of preservation. The edit must hold that weight. The name should be the last thing heard before the cut. Not a question. Not a reaction. The name.
The Nickname
"The story of 'Hajia 1 Day' — wherever it leads — is the episode's spine."
If the nickname opens a long story, the edit follows it. The arc structure is a framework, not a constraint. This episode's story may be in the name.
