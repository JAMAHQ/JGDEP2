Episode 002 — Location

New Location, New Story

Episode 002 shoots at a completely different location from Episode 001. The terracotta compound arch on Comodor Street is an interior world — it speaks of compound life, community, shelter, and the layered history of Jamestown's residential architecture. This must be reflected in how you light and frame the interview.

Episode 001 — Maame Osofo
Owuman Wall — Street-Facing, Ochre
Exterior. Public-facing. Golden sun rakes across from the west. Teal woodwork provides complementary accent. Open sky. The world of the trader, visible and present in her neighbourhood.
Episode 002 — Bla Quarter
Comodor Street Arch — Interior Compound, Terracotta
Semi-interior. The arch frames a deeper darkness behind. The terracotta wall is more saturated, more dramatic. Cracked tile floor. Drainpipe. Yellow jerrycans as found colour. A compound's private threshold — where family life began and ended each day.
Location Opportunity — The Arch Frame
Position Bla Quarter seated or standing just inside the arch mouth — not in front of it. This frames him in the threshold between the private world (the dark compound behind) and the public street. The arch becomes his frame within your frame. It is an extraordinary natural compositional device that no amount of production design can manufacture.
Colour Palette — What Changes From E001
The terracotta red wall is deeper, more saturated, and warmer than E001's ochre. The grade LUT base stays consistent — but the secondary grade for this episode will pull the reds slightly and let the deep arch shadow anchor the frame. The yellow jerrycans are a found accent; if they are present on shoot day, don't move them — they add life.

Born on 1st July 1960 on Comodor Street — within sight of the Jamestown Lighthouse — Bla Quarter has spent 65 years in Town E, one of Jamestown's most historically layered quarters. His working life bridged industrial labour at the powder factory and institutional service at the Judiciary. His family, the Kitson Mills, were teachers and educationalists — documented in the Sankofa books. That lineage makes him not just a witness to Jamestown's glory days, but a product of its intellectual tradition.

Why This Voice Matters
The Jamestown Royalty Dance is more than performance — it is a living expression of the history, identity and resilience of the Ga people. Bla Quarter's connection to this tradition makes him one of its cultural custodians. The dancers, elders and youth connected to the Royalty Dance all need their stories told. This episode begins that telling.
Key Stories to Draw Out
1. The Jamestown Royalty Dance — its meaning, its history, who held it alive
2. Life at the powder factory — what that industrial work looked like
3. The Judiciary as a caretaker — the institutional Jamestown he served
4. The Kitson Mills family — teachers and masters in the Sankofa record
5. What Town E (Lighthouse quarter) looked and felt like in its glory days
Physical Consideration — Eyesight
Eyesight is noted as a physical consideration. Ensure interview position does not require him to look into direct sunlight or a bright light source. The arch location naturally faces away from direct sun — use this. Confirm font size on any printed materials given to him. Have the production manager check in privately before the shoot.
Jamestown Glory Days — Mission

Youth Impact Framework

Bla Quarter's story carries a specific and powerful message for the youth of Jamestown — that cultural identity is not inherited passively. It is carried, practiced, and handed forward deliberately.

Episode 002 · Youth Mission Statement
The Dance Is the Archive. You Are the Dancer.
The Jamestown Royalty Dance is not a relic. It is a living document — written in the bodies of those who practice it and passed forward only through human chain. Bla Quarter's family, the Kitson Mills, were teachers and educationalists. His people understood that knowledge must be transmitted deliberately, or it is lost. The youth of Jamestown are the next link in that chain — for the Royalty Dance, for Ga cultural identity, and for the intellectual tradition of a community that has always known the value of learning. This episode asks them: what are you carrying forward?
Cultural Identity Ga Heritage The Royalty Dance Educational Lineage Community Custodianship Lighthouse Quarter Pride
The Culture Thread
Ask Bla Quarter to describe the Royalty Dance as if the viewer has never seen it — its movements, its sounds, its occasion, its meaning. This description becomes the episode's centrepiece. Let him use his hands and body if he begins to demonstrate — keep cameras rolling.
The Education Thread
The Kitson Mills family are in the Sankofa books as teachers and educationalists. This is documented Jamestown intellectual heritage. Ask him what his family believed about education — and what that meant for the children of his community.
End Card Direction
Final question: "What do you want the young people of Jamestown to know about the Royalty Dance — and about who they are?" His answer is the episode's closing frame. Direct address to youth. Keep cameras rolling after he finishes.
Archive Opportunity
The Sankofa books reference is significant. If accessible, footage of or reference to those documents as B-roll would be extraordinary. Ask Bla Quarter if he knows where they are held. This is a research thread worth pursuing before shoot day.
Episode 002 — Story Arc

Three-Part Mini-Arc

Bla Quarter's arc follows the series template — Past, Faith/Family/Community, Legacy/Youth — but shaped entirely around his specific stories: the Royalty Dance as the spine, the Kitson Mills educational heritage as the undertow, and the Lighthouse quarter as the setting.

A man born in the shadow of the Lighthouse, who spent his working life between the powder factory and the palace of justice — and carried in his body a dance that is older than either.

Cultural Wardrobe Note
As a male elder from the Ga community connected to the Royalty Dance, Bla Quarter may own traditional Ga garments — kente or smock in appropriate colours. If he has a garment associated with the dance itself, discuss with him whether it is appropriate to wear during the interview. The connection to the Royalty Dance worn on his body would be a powerful visual statement. Do not push — let him decide.

Golden Hour · Comodor Street Arch

Camera & Light Schematic

The arch location changes the lighting challenge significantly from E001. The subject sits in a threshold — one side lit by ambient golden sun, the other falling into the deep compound shadow. This tension is the visual poetry of the shot. Do not fight it. Work with it.

ARCH WALL [ terracotta red · peeling plaster · arched opening ] | DEEP SHADOW | <-- compound interior behind subject | | [ RIM light ] | [ SUBJECT ] | <-- seated at arch threshold LED warm | Bla Quarter | camera-right | | v [ GOLDEN SUN — enters arch mouth from the west at golden hour ] Rakes across terracotta face · subject lit from camera-left naturally [ REFLECTOR — bounces sun back into shadow side · camera-right ] [ A-CAM · BMPCC 6K · 85mm · f/2.2 · on sticks ] [ B-CAM · Lumix S5 · 85mm · f/2.0 · 30° offset ] [ KEY: expose for face — let arch shadow fall naturally ] [ the darkness behind subject is depth, not a problem ]
E002 Key Challenge
High contrast — bright terracotta wall vs. deep arch shadow. Expose for the subject's face. Do not try to light the interior. The shadow is the drama.
Eyesight Consideration
Subject has noted eyesight consideration. Position him so no light source is in his direct eyeline. The arch naturally faces away from sun — use this. Confirm with him before rolling.

Set Discipline — Non-Negotiable
Once cameras roll: no crew speaks. No phones. No movement unless critical. Hand signals only. If Bla Quarter begins to demonstrate the Royalty Dance — absolute silence and stillness from every crew member.
Royalty Dance Contingency
If at any point during the interview Bla Quarter begins to physically demonstrate the dance — do not stop him, do not redirect him. Signal the stills photographer to shoot. Signal B-cam operator to widen frame. This is the episode's most valuable potential moment.

Post-Production

Sound Design

For E002 specifically: if the Royalty Dance is described or demonstrated, the sound design team must research and source authentic Royalty Dance drumming and instrumentation. This episode may require deeper ethnomusicological audio research than E001.

1
Dialogue Bed
Clean, present, intimate. Boom primary; lav as emergency fill. Watch for arch reverb buildup on boom — monitor in real time.
Peaks at −18dBFS; 12dB headroom. Apply gentle EQ to manage any arch reverb if needed in post.
2
Comodor Street Ambient
Lighthouse quarter street sounds — different character from Owuman. Fishing boat horns, coastal birds, Comodor Street daily rhythm.
Independent ambient recorder running all day. This bed distinguishes E002 from E001 sonically.
3
Royalty Dance — Sound Research
The Royalty Dance has specific drumming and vocal traditions. Source authentic recordings or commission recording with custodians. This is the sonic centrepiece of the episode.
Research before shoot day. Consult Bla Quarter himself — he may know where recordings exist.
4
Methodist Church Acoustics
When he speaks of the church — a subtle reverb tail opens under his voice. Different character from the Divine Healers reverb in E001.
Use a Methodist church or stone building IR. Pull back as he returns to present.
5
Memory Washes
4–8 bar motif from series composer. The Royalty Dance theme may inform this episode's specific version of the series motif.
Enters at 10% under dialogue; rises only after he stops speaking. 3–4 uses per episode maximum.
6
Silence as Design
If he demonstrates the Royalty Dance — after he stops, 3 seconds of absolute silence before any cut. Let the air carry it.
The dance moment must breathe. Do not rush the edit away from it.
7
Series Motif — E002 Version
The 4-note Jamestown motif returns from E001, but in E002 it may incorporate a percussive element drawn from Royalty Dance instrumentation.
Composer decision — brief early in Part II, full resolution in Part III closing.
8
Youth Address End Card
"You are the dancer now." — the final frame. Dry mix, full presence, no reverb. The elder is speaking directly to the viewer.
Underscore fades completely. His voice alone. Then the series motif — and black.

Post-Production

Editorial Principles

All series editorial principles apply to E002. Additional E002 note: if the Royalty Dance is demonstrated during interview, that sequence must be edited with extreme care — it is the most culturally significant material in the episode and must not be reduced to a brief cutaway.

The Held Cut
"Hold interview frames 1.5× longer than instinct says."
Emotion lives in the face after words end. Cutting too early wastes the most valuable footage.
The Breath Cut
"Cut on the exhale, not the inhale."
Follows the natural rhythm of speech. Cuts on exhales feel invisible to the viewer.
The Silence Cut
"Never cut during a pause — wait for the next word, then cut back."
Pauses are the subject thinking. Cutting mid-pause breaks the connection to that thought.
Dance Sequence
"If the Royalty Dance is demonstrated — do not cut during it. Hold for the full duration."
This is a living cultural document. You cannot cut the living document. Let it complete. Then hold.
Youth End Card
"Every episode ends with the elder's direct address to Jamestown's youth."
This is the series mission made explicit. It is not optional. It is the reason we are making this.
Music Entry
"Music enters at 10% under dialogue and rises only after the subject stops speaking."
Music must never compete with the elder's voice. His words are primary.
Episode Endings
"Every episode ends on minimum 8 seconds of black."
Allow the audience to sit with what they heard before anything else demands their attention.
